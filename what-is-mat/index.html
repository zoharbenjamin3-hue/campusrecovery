What Is Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)?

MAT combines FDA-approved medications with counseling and behavioral therapies to provide a whole-patient approach to treating substance use disorders.

Home › Resources › What Is MAT?
Author: Daniel Touloumis
Editor: Devin Scheller, Director of Operations
Medical Reviewer: Sahil Talwar, PA
Published: August 1, 2024
Last Edited: March 29, 2026

Medication-Assisted Treatment, commonly known as MAT, is an evidence-based approach to treating substance use disorders that combines FDA-approved medications with counseling and behavioral therapies. Rather than relying on medication or therapy alone, MAT integrates both to address the biological, psychological, and social dimensions of addiction. Research consistently shows that MAT improves patient survival, increases retention in treatment, and reduces illicit drug use and criminal activity.

How MAT Works

Addiction changes brain chemistry in profound ways. Substances like opioids and alcohol alter the brain’s reward system, creating powerful cravings and physical dependence that make it extraordinarily difficult to stop using through willpower alone. MAT medications work at the neurological level to normalize brain chemistry, block the euphoric effects of substances, relieve physiological cravings, and stabilize body functions — all without producing the high associated with the abused substance.

When combined with individual therapy, group counseling, and peer support, these medications create a stable foundation that allows individuals to fully engage in the therapeutic work necessary for long-term recovery. MAT is not substituting one drug for another — it is using carefully monitored, clinically appropriate medication to treat a chronic medical condition, much like using insulin to manage diabetes.

FDA-Approved MAT Medications

Several medications have received FDA approval for the treatment of opioid use disorder and alcohol use disorder. The choice of medication depends on the individual’s substance use history, medical condition, treatment goals, and clinical assessment.

Buprenorphine (Suboxone)

Suboxone is a combination of buprenorphine and naloxone used to treat opioid use disorder. Buprenorphine is a partial opioid agonist, meaning it activates opioid receptors in the brain to a much lesser degree than full agonists like heroin or fentanyl. This reduces cravings and withdrawal symptoms without producing significant euphoria. The naloxone component discourages misuse by precipitating withdrawal if the medication is injected rather than taken as prescribed (sublingually). Suboxone can be prescribed in office-based settings, making it accessible and practical for outpatient treatment.

Naltrexone (Oral and Vivitrol)

Naltrexone is an opioid antagonist that blocks the effects of opioids at the receptor level. If an individual takes opioids while on naltrexone, they will not experience the euphoric or sedating effects. Naltrexone is available in two forms: a daily oral tablet and Vivitrol, an extended-release injectable administered once per month. Vivitrol is particularly valuable because it eliminates the need for daily medication adherence, a common challenge in early recovery. Naltrexone is also FDA-approved for alcohol use disorder, where it reduces the rewarding effects of drinking and helps decrease cravings.

Other MAT Medications

Additional medications used in MAT include methadone (a full opioid agonist available only through certified opioid treatment programs), acamprosate (which helps restore brain chemical balance disrupted by chronic alcohol use), and disulfiram (which causes unpleasant reactions when alcohol is consumed, serving as a deterrent). The clinical team determines which medication — if any — is appropriate based on the individual’s unique needs.

The Evidence Behind MAT

MAT is endorsed by the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), the National Institute on Drug Abuse (NIDA), the World Health Organization (WHO), and the American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM). Decades of research support its effectiveness:

  • MAT has been shown to reduce opioid overdose deaths by approximately 50%
  • Patients receiving MAT are significantly more likely to remain in treatment compared to those receiving behavioral interventions alone
  • MAT decreases illicit opioid use, reduces HIV and hepatitis C transmission related to injection drug use, and improves social functioning
  • For alcohol use disorder, naltrexone and acamprosate significantly reduce heavy drinking days and improve abstinence rates
  • MAT combined with behavioral therapy produces better outcomes than either approach used independently

Despite this strong evidence base, misconceptions about MAT persist. Some believe it simply replaces one addiction with another. This is inaccurate. When taken as prescribed and combined with therapy, MAT medications do not produce intoxication or impairment. They allow the brain to heal while the individual develops the coping skills, support networks, and behavioral changes needed for sustained recovery.

Who Is a Candidate for MAT?

MAT is not appropriate for every individual, and it is never a standalone treatment. It is most commonly recommended for individuals with:

  • Opioid use disorder — including addiction to prescription painkillers, heroin, or fentanyl
  • Alcohol use disorder — particularly moderate to severe cases where cravings are a primary relapse trigger
  • A history of relapse despite previous treatment attempts
  • Co-occurring mental health conditions that complicate recovery
  • High-risk factors for overdose, including recent relapse after a period of abstinence

The decision to use MAT is made collaboratively between the client and their treatment team. It is based on a comprehensive medical and psychiatric evaluation, not applied as a default for all clients.

MAT at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center in Lake Worth Beach, Florida, integrates MAT into our outpatient treatment programs when clinically indicated. Our medical team, led by experienced clinicians and overseen by a physician assistant, evaluates each client to determine whether medication would benefit their recovery plan.

We offer:

  • Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone) for opioid use disorder
  • Vivitrol (extended-release injectable naltrexone) for opioid and alcohol use disorders
  • Oral naltrexone for opioid and alcohol use disorders
  • Ongoing medication monitoring and dosage adjustments
  • Integration with individual therapy, group counseling, and psychiatric care

MAT is available across all our levels of care, including PHP and IOP. Medications are prescribed based on medical necessity, monitored regularly, and adjusted as the client progresses through treatment. Our goal is to use medication as a bridge that supports the client while they build the skills and support systems necessary for long-term recovery.

If you or someone you love is struggling with opioid or alcohol addiction, MAT may be an important part of the recovery plan. Contact Campus Recovery Center to speak with our admissions team about whether MAT is right for you.
