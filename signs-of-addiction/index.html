Signs of Addiction: How to Recognize Substance Use Disorder

Addiction often develops gradually. Recognizing the warning signs early can save a life and open the door to effective treatment.

Home › Resources › Signs of Addiction
Author: Daniel Touloumis
Editor: Devin Scheller, Director of Operations
Medical Reviewer: Sahil Talwar, PA
Published: August 1, 2024
Last Edited: March 29, 2026

Substance use disorder (SUD) is a chronic, treatable medical condition that affects the brain’s reward system, motivation, and decision-making circuitry. It does not discriminate by age, gender, income, or background. According to the National Survey on Drug Use and Health, nearly 50 million Americans aged 12 and older had a substance use disorder in 2023. Yet many individuals and families struggle to identify when casual or occasional use has crossed the line into a disorder that requires professional treatment.

Understanding the signs of addiction is the first step toward getting help. The warning signs generally fall into three categories: physical, behavioral, and psychological.

Physical Signs of Addiction

Substance use takes a measurable toll on the body. While specific symptoms vary by substance, the following physical changes are common across many types of addiction:

  • Changes in appearance — unexplained weight loss or gain, bloodshot eyes, dilated or constricted pupils, skin changes, poor hygiene, or a generally unkempt appearance
  • Sleep disturbances — insomnia, sleeping excessively, or erratic sleep patterns
  • Tremors or shakiness — particularly noticeable in the hands, often a sign of withdrawal from alcohol or sedatives
  • Frequent illness — a weakened immune system leading to colds, infections, or slow-healing wounds
  • Tolerance — needing more of a substance to achieve the same effect, indicating the brain has adapted to the drug’s presence
  • Withdrawal symptoms — experiencing nausea, sweating, anxiety, agitation, or seizures when the substance is not available
  • Changes in appetite — eating significantly more or less than usual
  • Unexplained injuries — bruises, cuts, or accidents that the person cannot adequately explain

Behavioral Signs of Addiction

Changes in behavior are often the most visible indicators that something has shifted. Loved ones, coworkers, and friends may notice these patterns before the individual does:

  • Secrecy and isolation — withdrawing from family and friends, spending time with new and unknown associates, or being evasive about whereabouts and activities
  • Neglecting responsibilities — declining performance at work or school, missing deadlines, calling in sick frequently, or failing to meet family obligations
  • Financial problems — unexplained spending, borrowing money, selling possessions, or stealing to fund substance use
  • Loss of interest — abandoning hobbies, sports, social activities, or other pursuits that were once meaningful
  • Risky behavior — driving under the influence, engaging in unsafe sexual activity, or putting oneself in dangerous situations
  • Legal issues — arrests for DUI, possession, disorderly conduct, or other substance-related offenses
  • Failed attempts to stop — repeatedly expressing a desire to cut back or quit but being unable to follow through
  • Hiding substance use — concealing bottles, pills, or paraphernalia, or using substances in secret

Psychological Signs of Addiction

Addiction profoundly affects mental and emotional health. Many psychological symptoms overlap with mental health conditions like depression and anxiety, which is why professional assessment is essential:

  • Mood swings — rapid shifts between euphoria, irritability, anger, and depression
  • Increased anxiety or paranoia — heightened nervousness, fear, or suspiciousness that was not present before
  • Depression and hopelessness — persistent sadness, loss of motivation, or feelings that things will never improve
  • Defensiveness — becoming hostile or aggressive when substance use is mentioned
  • Denial — minimizing the extent of use, rationalizing behavior, or insisting there is no problem
  • Cognitive impairment — difficulty concentrating, memory lapses, confusion, or impaired judgment
  • Lack of motivation — a general apathy toward life, goals, and self-care
  • Obsessive thinking — spending excessive time thinking about obtaining, using, or recovering from substances

When Does Substance Use Become a Disorder?

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) defines substance use disorder along a spectrum from mild to severe, based on 11 diagnostic criteria. These include impaired control over use, social impairment, risky use, and pharmacological indicators like tolerance and withdrawal. An individual meeting 2–3 criteria has a mild SUD, 4–5 criteria indicates moderate SUD, and 6 or more is classified as severe.

It is not necessary to “hit rock bottom” before seeking treatment. In fact, early intervention consistently produces better outcomes. If you recognize several of the signs described above in yourself or a loved one, it may be time to seek a professional evaluation.

How to Approach a Loved One

Confronting someone about their substance use is one of the most difficult conversations a family member or friend can have. These guidelines can help:

  • Choose the right time — approach the person when they are sober, calm, and in a private setting
  • Use “I” statements — express your concern in terms of how their behavior affects you, rather than making accusations (e.g., “I’m worried about you” rather than “You have a problem”)
  • Be specific — reference concrete examples of behavior changes you have observed
  • Listen without judgment — the goal is to open a dialogue, not to win an argument
  • Offer solutions — have treatment options ready to share, including a phone number they can call
  • Set boundaries — make clear what you will and will not tolerate, and follow through consistently
  • Seek professional guidance — consider consulting an interventionist or therapist who specializes in addiction if initial conversations are unsuccessful
