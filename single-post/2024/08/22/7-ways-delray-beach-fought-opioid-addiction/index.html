7 Ways Delray Beach Fought Opioid Addiction

How a South Florida community coordinated efforts to combat the opioid crisis and achieved a 60% reduction in overdoses.

Home › News & Updates › 7 Ways Delray Beach Fought Opioid Addiction
Author: Daniel
Editor: Devin Scheller, Director of Operations
Published: August 22, 2024
Last Edited: March 29, 2026
Category: Uncategorized

Delray Beach, Florida—a city of approximately 67,000 residents—once hosted 122 treatment facilities and experienced overdose increases exceeding 250% year over year. Through coordinated community action involving city officials, treatment providers, law enforcement, and everyday residents, the city achieved an approximately 60% drop in overdoses between October 2016 and October 2017. Here are the seven key strategies that made the difference.

1. Outreach Specialist Program

Delray Beach deployed dedicated outreach specialists who connected directly with individuals struggling with addiction in the community. Rather than waiting for people to seek help, these specialists went into the field—visiting shelters, parks, and other locations where individuals in crisis could be reached. This proactive approach helped bridge the gap between people in need and available treatment services.

2. Law Enforcement Narcan Distribution

Local law enforcement officers were equipped with Narcan (naloxone), the opioid overdose reversal medication. By putting this life-saving tool directly in the hands of first responders, the city ensured that overdose victims could receive emergency treatment in the critical minutes before paramedics arrived. This initiative saved countless lives during the height of the crisis.

3. Sober Home Regulations

The city implemented zoning regulations requiring a minimum distance of 660 feet between sober living homes. This helped prevent the clustering of recovery housing in specific neighborhoods, reduced community impact, and improved the quality of the recovery environment for residents. The regulation balanced the need for sober housing with responsible community planning.

4. FARR Certification Requirements

Delray Beach pushed for sober homes to obtain certification through the Florida Association of Recovery Residences (FARR). FARR certification established minimum standards for safety, management, and operations of recovery housing, ensuring that individuals in early recovery had access to stable, well-managed living environments.

5. Sober Home Task Force

A dedicated task force was established to investigate and prosecute operators of fraudulent or exploitative sober homes. This enforcement effort resulted in 45 arrests, sending a clear message that predatory practices targeting vulnerable individuals in recovery would not be tolerated. The task force helped clean up an industry that had been plagued by bad actors.

6. Industry Collaboration

Treatment providers, city officials, law enforcement, and community organizations came together in an unprecedented collaborative effort. Rather than operating in silos, these stakeholders shared information, coordinated strategies, and held each other accountable. This collaborative approach ensured that anti-addiction efforts were comprehensive and mutually reinforcing.

7. Community Recovery Infrastructure

Delray Beach invested in building a robust recovery support infrastructure, including a thriving network of 12-step meetings and peer support groups. At the height of these efforts, nearly 5,000 people attended weekly 12-step meetings in the area. This community-level support system provided the ongoing connections and accountability essential for long-term recovery.

The Results

Through these combined strategies, Delray Beach achieved a dramatic reduction in overdose incidents. The approximately 60% decline in overdoses between October 2016 and October 2017 demonstrated that a coordinated, multi-faceted approach—combining outreach, regulation, enforcement, collaboration, and community support—can make a meaningful impact on the opioid crisis.

The Delray Beach model serves as a blueprint for communities across the country facing similar challenges. It shows that progress requires action from every sector: government, healthcare, law enforcement, and the community itself.
