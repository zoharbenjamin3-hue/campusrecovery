Can The Addiction Industry Be Taught Regulations From The Kidney Industry?

Exploring how the dialysis industry's comprehensive oversight framework could serve as a model for addiction treatment regulation.

Last Edited: March 29, 2026

The addiction treatment industry in the United States is a $40 billion sector with over 14,000 facilities. Despite its enormous scale and the critical nature of the services it provides, the industry faces fragmented, state-by-state regulation that varies widely in scope and enforcement. Meanwhile, the kidney and dialysis industry has built a comprehensive, federally coordinated oversight framework that could serve as a model for addiction treatment.

The Current State of Addiction Treatment Regulation

Addiction treatment in the United States is regulated primarily at the state level, resulting in a patchwork of standards that differ significantly from one jurisdiction to another. While some states have implemented robust licensing requirements and oversight mechanisms, others have minimal regulation. This inconsistency creates gaps that can affect patient safety, treatment quality, and accountability across the industry.

The absence of universal standards means that consumers often lack the tools to evaluate and compare treatment facilities objectively. Unlike many other areas of healthcare, there is no standardized national rating system, no mandatory outcome reporting, and no financial consequences for facilities that consistently deliver poor results.

The Kidney Industry's Regulatory Framework

The kidney and dialysis industry offers a starkly different picture. Since 1972, when Medicare coverage was extended universally to patients with End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD), the federal government has built an extensive oversight infrastructure for dialysis care. Key components include:

CMS Certification

The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) requires all dialysis facilities to meet federal certification standards. This creates a baseline of quality and safety that applies uniformly across the country, regardless of state.

HSAG Quality Monitoring

Health Services Advisory Group (HSAG) conducts independent quality monitoring of dialysis facilities, providing an external layer of accountability that helps identify underperforming facilities and drive improvement.

DFC Star Program

The Dialysis Facility Compare (DFC) Star Program rates facilities on a 1-to-5 star scale based on quality metrics. These ratings are publicly available, giving patients transparent, objective information to guide their healthcare decisions.

ESRD QIP

The ESRD Quality Incentive Program (QIP) ties financial penalties to quality performance. Facilities that fail to meet established quality benchmarks face reductions in their Medicare reimbursement rates—creating a direct financial incentive to maintain high standards of care.

CAHPS Patient Surveys

Standardized Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems (CAHPS) surveys collect patient feedback on their care experience. These surveys provide another dimension of quality measurement, centering the patient voice in quality evaluation.

Lessons for the Addiction Treatment Industry

Several elements of the kidney industry's regulatory framework could be adapted to improve oversight and accountability in addiction treatment:

Independent Quality Monitoring

Establishing an independent entity to monitor and evaluate addiction treatment facilities—similar to HSAG's role in dialysis care—would provide objective, external quality assessment. This would help identify facilities that are underperforming and create accountability beyond the self-reporting that currently characterizes much of the industry.

Outcome-Based Metrics

Developing standardized, outcome-based quality metrics for addiction treatment would allow for meaningful comparison between facilities. Metrics could include treatment completion rates, post-treatment sobriety rates, patient satisfaction scores, and measures of functional improvement.

Standardized Patient Surveys

Implementing CAHPS-style patient experience surveys across addiction treatment facilities would center patient perspectives in quality evaluation and give consumers reliable information for making treatment decisions.

Financial Consequences for Poor Performance

Creating a system of financial incentives and penalties tied to quality outcomes—similar to the ESRD QIP—would give facilities a direct economic motivation to prioritize quality care. This approach shifts the incentive structure from volume to value.

Public Facility Ratings

A publicly accessible rating system for addiction treatment facilities, comparable to the DFC Star Program, would empower consumers with transparent, standardized information and create competitive pressure for quality improvement.

Balancing Regulation and Accessibility

While the kidney industry model offers valuable lessons, it is important to recognize that overly enforcement-focused regulatory approaches can have unintended consequences. Excessive regulatory burden can discourage qualified healthcare professionals from entering the addiction treatment field, reduce the number of available treatment providers, and ultimately limit access to care for individuals who desperately need it.

The goal should be a balanced framework that establishes meaningful quality standards and accountability mechanisms while ensuring that the regulatory environment encourages—rather than discourages—high-quality professionals and facilities from serving this population.

Moving Forward

The addiction treatment industry has made significant progress in recent years, particularly in states like Florida that have implemented landmark regulatory legislation. But there is still much to learn from industries that have built more mature, comprehensive oversight systems. By adapting the best elements of the kidney industry's regulatory framework—independent monitoring, outcome-based metrics, patient surveys, financial incentives, and public transparency—the addiction treatment industry can continue its evolution toward the standard of care that its patients deserve.
