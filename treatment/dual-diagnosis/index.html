Dual diagnosis—the co-occurrence of a substance use disorder and one or more mental health conditions—is far more common than many people realize. According to the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), approximately 9.2 million adults in the United States have both a substance use disorder and a mental illness. Despite this prevalence, the majority of individuals with co-occurring disorders receive treatment for only one condition or no treatment at all. Campus Recovery Center provides integrated dual diagnosis treatment at our Joint Commission-accredited facility in Lake Worth Beach, Florida, addressing both conditions simultaneously for the best possible outcomes.

Understanding Dual Diagnosis

Dual diagnosis, also called co-occurring disorders, refers to the simultaneous presence of a substance use disorder and a mental health disorder. The relationship between these conditions is complex and bidirectional. Mental health conditions can lead to substance use as individuals attempt to self-medicate their symptoms, while substance use can trigger or worsen mental health disorders by disrupting brain chemistry and neural pathways.

Common co-occurring combinations include:

  • Alcohol use disorder and depression: Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant that can initially alleviate depressive symptoms but ultimately worsens them through neurochemical disruption
  • Opioid addiction and PTSD: Many individuals with trauma histories use opioids to numb emotional pain, creating a cycle of dependence that compounds the original trauma
  • Stimulant use and anxiety disorders: Cocaine and methamphetamine use can trigger severe anxiety, panic attacks, and paranoia, while some individuals with ADHD misuse stimulants attempting to self-treat attention deficits
  • Benzodiazepine dependence and generalized anxiety disorder: Prescribed for anxiety, benzodiazepines can create physical dependence that ultimately worsens the anxiety they were meant to treat
  • Substance use and bipolar disorder: Individuals with bipolar disorder have significantly elevated rates of substance use disorder, often using substances to manage manic or depressive episodes
  • Polysubstance use and borderline personality disorder: Emotional dysregulation and impulsivity associated with BPD increase vulnerability to substance use disorders

Signs of Co-Occurring Disorders

Identifying co-occurring disorders can be challenging because the symptoms of substance use disorders and mental health conditions frequently overlap and interact. Key indicators include:

  • Using alcohol or drugs to cope with anxiety, depression, traumatic memories, or other emotional distress
  • Mental health symptoms that worsen during periods of substance use or withdrawal
  • History of treatment for addiction that has not addressed underlying mental health conditions
  • History of psychiatric treatment that has not addressed concurrent substance use
  • Repeated relapses despite completing addiction treatment programs
  • Psychiatric medications that seem ineffective (often because substance use undermines their efficacy)
  • Family history of both mental illness and substance use disorders
  • Onset of substance use following a traumatic event, major life stressor, or psychiatric episode
  • Significant mood instability, impulsivity, or personality changes beyond what substance use alone would explain
  • Suicidal ideation or self-harm behaviors in the context of substance use

Why Integrated Treatment Matters

Research consistently demonstrates that treating co-occurring disorders in an integrated fashion—addressing both the substance use disorder and the mental health condition within the same treatment program—produces significantly better outcomes than treating each condition separately. The traditional approach of treating addiction first and then addressing mental health (sequential treatment) or treating each condition with different providers who do not coordinate care (parallel treatment) has been shown to be less effective.

There are several reasons integrated treatment is superior:

  • Substance use and mental health conditions interact with and reinforce each other; treating only one leaves the other untreated and actively undermining recovery
  • A single treatment team can develop a unified plan that accounts for how both conditions affect each other
  • Medications can be selected and managed to address both conditions without worsening either
  • Therapies can be adapted to address the unique challenges that co-occurring disorders present
  • Clients do not receive contradictory messages from separate providers about their treatment

Treatment Programs at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center provides integrated dual diagnosis treatment across all levels of outpatient care. Our multidisciplinary treatment team includes licensed therapists, psychiatrists, physician assistants, and clinical support staff trained in co-occurring disorder treatment.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

PHP provides the most intensive outpatient support, with structured programming five to seven days per week. For dual diagnosis clients, PHP includes comprehensive psychiatric assessment, medication management addressing both conditions, daily individual and group therapy, trauma-informed care, and coordinated treatment planning. PHP is appropriate for individuals with acute psychiatric symptoms alongside active substance use disorders.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP provides structured dual diagnosis treatment three to five days per week while allowing clients to maintain daily responsibilities. The program includes integrated individual and group therapy, psychiatric medication management, skill-building sessions addressing both conditions, and relapse prevention planning that accounts for mental health triggers.

Outpatient Program (OP)

Standard outpatient care provides ongoing dual diagnosis support through individual therapy, group sessions, and psychiatric follow-up. OP serves as continuing care for individuals stepping down from more intensive programs or as standalone treatment for individuals with stabilized co-occurring conditions who need ongoing clinical support.

Evidence-Based Therapies for Dual Diagnosis

Campus Recovery Center uses therapeutic approaches specifically selected for their effectiveness in treating co-occurring disorders:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): CBT is effective for both substance use disorders and a wide range of mental health conditions including depression, anxiety, and PTSD. In dual diagnosis treatment, CBT helps clients identify the cognitive patterns that maintain both conditions, develop healthier coping strategies, and break the cycle of using substances to manage psychiatric symptoms.
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): DBT is particularly effective for dual diagnosis clients, especially those with emotional dysregulation, borderline personality features, or trauma histories. The four skill modules—mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotional regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness—address the core deficits that drive both substance use and psychiatric instability.
  • Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): For clients with opioid or alcohol use disorders, MAT medications such as buprenorphine, naltrexone, or acamprosate can be safely combined with psychiatric medications to address both conditions. Our psychiatric team manages all medications within a unified treatment plan.
  • Psychiatric Medication Management: Many co-occurring mental health conditions require medication. Our psychiatric providers prescribe and monitor medications for depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, PTSD, and other conditions, carefully selecting agents that are safe and effective for individuals with substance use disorders.
  • Trauma-Informed Care: Trauma is a common thread connecting substance use and mental health disorders. Our clinicians use trauma-informed approaches, including EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) concepts and trauma-focused CBT, to address trauma without retraumatization.
  • Group Therapy: Dual diagnosis group sessions bring together individuals who share the experience of managing both addiction and mental illness, reducing shame and isolation while building practical coping skills.
  • Motivational Interviewing: Individuals with co-occurring disorders often experience complex ambivalence about treatment. MI helps clients explore their relationship with substances and mental health treatment, strengthening internal motivation for recovery.

Common Co-Occurring Mental Health Conditions We Treat

Campus Recovery Center provides integrated treatment for substance use disorders co-occurring with:

  • Major Depressive Disorder: Persistent sadness, loss of interest, sleep disturbance, and hopelessness that frequently co-occurs with alcohol and opioid use disorders
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder: Chronic, excessive worry and physical anxiety symptoms often self-medicated with alcohol, benzodiazepines, or cannabis
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Trauma-related flashbacks, hypervigilance, and emotional numbing frequently associated with substance use as a coping mechanism
  • Bipolar Disorder: Mood cycling between mania and depression, with significantly elevated rates of co-occurring substance use
  • Panic Disorder: Recurrent panic attacks that individuals may attempt to prevent or manage through substance use
  • Borderline Personality Disorder: Emotional instability, impulsivity, and interpersonal difficulties that increase vulnerability to substance use disorders
  • ADHD: Attention deficits and impulsivity that increase the risk of substance misuse, particularly stimulant and alcohol use disorders

Recovery Outlook

Dual diagnosis is a complex clinical presentation, but recovery is achievable with appropriate integrated treatment. Research shows that individuals who receive integrated care for both conditions have better treatment retention, lower relapse rates, improved psychiatric stability, and better overall quality of life compared to those who receive treatment for only one condition.

Recovery from co-occurring disorders is typically a longer-term process than recovery from either condition alone, which is why Campus Recovery Center emphasizes continuity of care across treatment levels. Clients progress from PHP to IOP to OP as their clinical status improves, maintaining therapeutic relationships and treatment consistency throughout their recovery journey.
