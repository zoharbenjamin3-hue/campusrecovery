Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a serious mental health condition that develops after exposure to a traumatic or life-threatening event. Approximately 6 percent of the U.S. population will experience PTSD at some point in their lives, and about 12 million adults have PTSD in any given year. PTSD does not resolve on its own—it requires professional treatment. At Campus Recovery Center in Lake Worth Beach, Florida, we provide trauma-focused outpatient programs that help individuals process traumatic experiences, reduce debilitating symptoms, and reclaim their sense of safety and control.

What Is PTSD?

PTSD is a trauma- and stressor-related disorder that can develop after a person experiences, witnesses, or learns about a traumatic event involving actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. While it is normal to experience acute stress reactions after a traumatic event, PTSD is diagnosed when symptoms persist for more than one month and cause significant impairment in daily functioning.

Traumatic events that commonly lead to PTSD include military combat, sexual assault, physical abuse, serious accidents, natural disasters, childhood abuse or neglect, and witnessing violence. However, PTSD can result from any event that overwhelms an individual's capacity to cope. Not everyone who experiences trauma develops PTSD—risk factors include the severity and duration of the trauma, prior trauma history, genetic predisposition, lack of social support, and co-occurring mental health conditions.

Signs and Symptoms of PTSD

The DSM-5 organizes PTSD symptoms into four clusters, all of which must be present for a diagnosis:

  • Intrusion symptoms – Recurrent, involuntary, and distressing memories of the traumatic event; flashbacks in which the person feels as though the event is happening again; nightmares related to the trauma; intense psychological distress or physical reactions when exposed to trauma reminders
  • Avoidance – Persistent efforts to avoid internal reminders (thoughts, feelings, memories) and external reminders (people, places, activities, situations) associated with the traumatic event
  • Negative changes in mood and cognition – Persistent negative beliefs about oneself or the world ("I am broken," "No one can be trusted"); distorted blame of self or others for the trauma; persistent negative emotions such as fear, horror, anger, guilt, or shame; diminished interest in activities; feeling detached from others; inability to experience positive emotions
  • Hyperarousal and reactivity – Irritable or aggressive behavior; reckless or self-destructive behavior; hypervigilance; exaggerated startle response; difficulty concentrating; sleep disturbances

How PTSD Impacts Daily Life

PTSD profoundly disrupts daily functioning across every domain of life. The constant state of hypervigilance is physically and emotionally exhausting, making it difficult to concentrate, perform at work or school, and engage in routine activities. Flashbacks and intrusive memories can be triggered by sights, sounds, smells, or situations that bear any resemblance to the original trauma, causing individuals to feel as though they are reliving the event.

Avoidance behaviors progressively limit an individual's world as they try to steer clear of anything that might trigger distressing memories. Relationships suffer as emotional numbing, irritability, and detachment make it difficult to connect with loved ones. Sleep disruption from nightmares and hyperarousal leads to chronic exhaustion. Many individuals with PTSD also experience physical symptoms including chronic pain, headaches, gastrointestinal problems, and cardiovascular issues related to sustained stress activation.

PTSD Treatment Programs at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center offers trauma-informed care at multiple levels of outpatient treatment. Our clinical team develops individualized treatment plans that account for the nature and severity of each person's trauma, their symptom presentation, and any co-occurring conditions.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

Our PHP provides intensive, structured treatment five to seven days per week for individuals with severe PTSD symptoms that significantly impair daily functioning. PHP includes daily individual trauma-focused therapy, process groups, psychiatric evaluation, medication management, and skills training. This level of care provides maximum clinical support while allowing clients to return home in the evening.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP offers structured treatment three to five days per week for individuals who need more support than traditional outpatient therapy but can maintain some daily responsibilities. IOP includes trauma-focused group therapy, individual counseling, coping skills development, and psychoeducation about trauma and recovery. IOP also serves as a step-down from PHP.

Outpatient Program (OP)

Our outpatient program provides individual trauma therapy, group sessions, and psychiatric services on a flexible schedule. OP is designed for individuals with stabilized PTSD symptoms who benefit from ongoing professional support, or those transitioning from more intensive treatment levels.

Evidence-Based Therapies for PTSD

Our clinical team uses therapies that are specifically recommended by the American Psychological Association and the Department of Veterans Affairs for treating PTSD:

  • Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) – CPT helps individuals examine and challenge distorted beliefs that developed as a result of trauma, such as self-blame, mistrust, and beliefs about safety and control. Through structured sessions, clients learn to process the trauma and develop a more balanced understanding of their experience.
  • Prolonged Exposure Therapy (PE) – PE involves the gradual, repeated confrontation of trauma-related memories, feelings, and situations that have been avoided since the traumatic event. By processing these experiences in a safe therapeutic environment, the power of the trauma memory diminishes over time.
  • Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) – EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (such as guided eye movements) while the client recalls traumatic memories, helping the brain reprocess the trauma so it is no longer experienced with the same emotional intensity.
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) – DBT equips individuals with skills for managing intense emotions, tolerating distress, and improving interpersonal relationships. DBT is especially valuable for individuals whose trauma has led to emotional dysregulation or self-destructive behaviors.
  • Medication Management – Our psychiatric providers prescribe and monitor medications when clinically appropriate, including SSRIs such as sertraline and paroxetine, which are FDA-approved for PTSD treatment, as well as prazosin for trauma-related nightmares and other medications as indicated.
  • Group Therapy – Trauma-focused group therapy provides a safe space for individuals with PTSD to share experiences, reduce isolation, learn coping strategies, and realize they are not alone in their struggle. Group support is a powerful complement to individual trauma therapy.

PTSD and Co-Occurring Substance Use

PTSD and substance use disorders are among the most commonly co-occurring conditions in behavioral health. Research indicates that nearly half of individuals with PTSD also meet the criteria for a substance use disorder. Many people with PTSD turn to alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines, or cannabis to numb traumatic memories, reduce hyperarousal, and manage sleep disruption. While substances may provide short-term relief, they prevent proper trauma processing, worsen PTSD symptoms over time, and create a dangerous cycle of dependence.

Campus Recovery Center specializes in dual diagnosis treatment, providing integrated care that addresses both PTSD and substance use simultaneously. Our clinical team understands the complex relationship between trauma and addiction, and our treatment approach ensures that both conditions are treated concurrently for the best possible outcomes.

Recovery Outlook

PTSD is a treatable condition, and the majority of individuals who receive evidence-based treatment experience significant improvement. Research shows that CPT and PE produce meaningful symptom reduction in approximately 53 to 60 percent of patients, with many individuals no longer meeting diagnostic criteria for PTSD after completing treatment. Early intervention and comprehensive care improve outcomes, but recovery is possible at any stage.

At Campus Recovery Center, recovery from PTSD means more than symptom reduction—it means helping individuals rebuild their sense of safety, reconnect with others, and engage fully in the life they want to live. Healing from trauma is a courageous process, and our team is here to walk alongside you every step of the way.
