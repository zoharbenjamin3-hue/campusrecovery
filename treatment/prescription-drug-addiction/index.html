Prescription drug addiction is one of the fastest-growing forms of substance use disorder in the United States. According to the National Institute on Drug Abuse, an estimated 18 million Americans have misused prescription medications at least once in the past year. What often begins as legitimate medical treatment for pain, anxiety, or attention disorders can progress into physical dependence and addiction. Campus Recovery Center provides structured outpatient treatment for prescription drug addiction at our Joint Commission-accredited facility in Lake Worth Beach, Florida.

Understanding Prescription Drug Addiction

Prescription drug addiction refers to the compulsive use of prescription medications in ways other than prescribed—taking higher doses, using medications prescribed to someone else, or continuing to take medications after the medical need has resolved. The three classes of prescription drugs most commonly associated with addiction are:

  • Opioid painkillers: Oxycodone (OxyContin, Percocet), hydrocodone (Vicodin, Norco), codeine, morphine, and tramadol. These medications are prescribed for acute and chronic pain but carry a high risk of dependence and addiction.
  • Benzodiazepines and sedatives: Alprazolam (Xanax), diazepam (Valium), clonazepam (Klonopin), lorazepam (Ativan), and zolpidem (Ambien). Prescribed for anxiety and insomnia, these medications produce physical dependence rapidly, often within weeks of regular use.
  • Stimulants: Amphetamine (Adderall), methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta), and lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse). Prescribed for ADHD and narcolepsy, these medications increase dopamine activity and carry a risk of misuse, particularly among young adults.

Each class of prescription medication produces addiction through different neurobiological mechanisms, but all share a common pathway: the brain adapts to the presence of the drug, develops tolerance, and eventually requires the substance to maintain normal function. This is what makes prescription drug addiction a medical condition that requires professional treatment rather than willpower alone.

Signs and Symptoms of Prescription Drug Addiction

Prescription drug addiction can be difficult to identify because the initial use is medically sanctioned. Warning signs include:

  • Taking medication in higher doses or more frequently than prescribed
  • Continuing to take medication after the original condition has resolved
  • Visiting multiple doctors to obtain prescriptions (doctor shopping)
  • Using someone else’s prescription medication
  • Running out of prescriptions early
  • Requesting early refills or claiming prescriptions were lost or stolen
  • Changes in mood, energy, or sleep patterns
  • Declining performance at work or school
  • Social withdrawal and secretive behavior
  • Withdrawal symptoms when the medication is unavailable
  • Purchasing prescription drugs from illicit sources
  • Combining prescription medications with alcohol or other substances

Health Effects of Prescription Drug Misuse

The health consequences of prescription drug addiction vary by drug class but are universally serious:

  • Prescription opioids: Respiratory depression, constipation, hormonal disruption, immune suppression, hyperalgesia (increased pain sensitivity), overdose, and death. Long-term opioid use can worsen the pain it was originally prescribed to treat.
  • Benzodiazepines: Cognitive impairment, memory loss, increased fall risk, emotional blunting, paradoxical anxiety, potentially fatal withdrawal seizures, and respiratory depression when combined with opioids or alcohol
  • Prescription stimulants: Cardiovascular complications including elevated heart rate and blood pressure, arrhythmias, anxiety, paranoia, insomnia, weight loss, malnutrition, and in severe cases, stimulant-induced psychosis
  • Polysubstance risks: Many individuals with prescription drug addiction use multiple substances simultaneously, dramatically increasing the risk of dangerous drug interactions and fatal overdose

Treatment Programs at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center offers individualized treatment for prescription drug addiction across multiple levels of outpatient care. Because prescription drug addiction encompasses different drug classes with distinct pharmacological profiles, our treatment approach is tailored to each client’s specific substance, history, and clinical needs.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

PHP provides intensive clinical programming five to seven days per week. For prescription drug addiction, PHP includes daily psychiatric monitoring, medication management (including MAT for opioid dependence or supervised tapering for benzodiazepine dependence), individual and group therapy, and comprehensive clinical assessment. PHP provides the structure needed during early recovery when cravings and withdrawal are most intense.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP offers structured treatment three to five days per week while allowing clients to maintain daily responsibilities. The program includes evidence-based therapies, relapse prevention planning, medication management, and peer support. IOP is appropriate as a step-down from PHP or as initial treatment for individuals with moderate severity presentations.

Outpatient Program (OP)

Standard outpatient care provides ongoing clinical support through individual therapy, group counseling, and psychiatric services on a flexible schedule. OP serves as continuing care for individuals who have completed more intensive treatment or as standalone care for those with less severe presentations.

Evidence-Based Treatment Approaches

Campus Recovery Center uses clinically validated therapies tailored to the specific class of prescription drug involved:

  • Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): For prescription opioid addiction, MAT with buprenorphine or naltrexone is the clinical standard of care. These medications reduce cravings, prevent withdrawal, and significantly improve treatment retention and outcomes. For benzodiazepine dependence, our psychiatric team develops individualized tapering protocols.
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): CBT helps clients identify the thought patterns, beliefs, and situations that trigger prescription drug misuse. Clients learn to challenge cognitive distortions, develop healthy coping mechanisms, and build skills for managing pain, anxiety, or attention difficulties without reliance on addictive medications.
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): DBT provides skills in emotional regulation, distress tolerance, mindfulness, and interpersonal effectiveness that are essential for individuals who have used prescription drugs to manage emotional pain or anxiety.
  • Motivational Interviewing: Many individuals with prescription drug addiction experience significant ambivalence about stopping medication that was originally prescribed by a doctor. Motivational interviewing helps resolve this ambivalence and strengthen commitment to recovery.
  • Group Therapy: Group sessions provide peer support and reduce the shame and stigma that often accompany prescription drug addiction. Hearing from others who have navigated similar challenges normalizes the recovery experience.
  • Pain Management Education: For individuals with co-occurring chronic pain, our team provides education on non-addictive pain management strategies, including physical therapy referrals, non-opioid medications, and mind-body approaches.
  • Family Education: Prescription drug addiction affects the entire family. Our clinicians provide family education to help loved ones understand the nature of addiction, establish healthy boundaries, and support the recovery process.

Dual Diagnosis Treatment

Prescription drug addiction frequently co-occurs with the conditions the medications were originally prescribed to treat. Chronic pain, anxiety disorders, insomnia, ADHD, and depression are common co-occurring diagnoses. Campus Recovery Center provides integrated dual diagnosis treatment that addresses both the addiction and the underlying condition simultaneously. Our clinicians work with each client to develop alternative treatment strategies for their original condition that do not carry the risk of addiction.

Recovery Outlook

Prescription drug addiction is a treatable condition with strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of structured treatment programs. Recovery outcomes are particularly favorable when treatment includes medication-assisted approaches (for opioid addiction), addresses co-occurring conditions, provides education on alternative management strategies for the original medical condition, and includes ongoing aftercare support.

Campus Recovery Center helps clients build a foundation for lasting recovery by developing the clinical tools, coping strategies, and support systems needed to manage their health without dependence on addictive prescription medications.
