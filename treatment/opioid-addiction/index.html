The opioid crisis remains one of the most devastating public health emergencies in American history. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, opioid-involved overdose deaths have continued at alarming rates, with synthetic opioids like fentanyl driving the majority of fatalities. Opioid use disorder (OUD) affects millions of Americans and requires specialized, evidence-based treatment to achieve sustained recovery. Campus Recovery Center provides comprehensive outpatient treatment for opioid addiction at our Joint Commission-accredited facility in Lake Worth Beach, Florida.

Understanding Opioid Use Disorder

Opioid use disorder is a chronic medical condition defined by the compulsive use of opioid substances despite harmful consequences. Opioids include prescription painkillers such as oxycodone, hydrocodone, and morphine, as well as illicit substances like heroin and illicitly manufactured fentanyl. These substances bind to opioid receptors in the brain, producing powerful pain relief and euphoria while simultaneously altering the brain’s reward circuitry.

With repeated exposure, the brain develops tolerance, requiring increasing amounts of the substance to achieve the same effect. Physical dependence follows, meaning the body cannot function normally without the drug. When opioid use is reduced or stopped, the individual experiences withdrawal—a constellation of intensely uncomfortable symptoms that drives continued use. This neurobiological cycle is what distinguishes opioid use disorder from voluntary drug use and why professional treatment is essential.

Signs and Symptoms of Opioid Addiction

Opioid use disorder manifests through behavioral, physical, and psychological symptoms:

  • Taking opioids in larger amounts or over a longer period than intended
  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down opioid use
  • Spending excessive time obtaining, using, or recovering from opioids
  • Intense cravings for opioids
  • Failure to fulfill major obligations at work, school, or home
  • Continued opioid use despite persistent social or relationship problems
  • Withdrawal from social, occupational, or recreational activities
  • Using opioids in physically dangerous situations
  • Continued use despite knowing opioids are causing physical or psychological harm
  • Tolerance—needing markedly increased doses
  • Withdrawal symptoms including muscle aches, insomnia, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, dilated pupils, goosebumps, and severe anxiety

Health Effects of Opioid Abuse

Chronic opioid misuse produces serious medical complications that affect multiple body systems:

  • Respiratory depression: Opioids suppress the brain’s respiratory drive, which is the primary mechanism of fatal overdose. Even non-fatal respiratory depression can cause hypoxic brain injury.
  • Infectious disease: Injection drug use increases the risk of HIV, hepatitis B and C, endocarditis, and soft tissue infections
  • Gastrointestinal dysfunction: Chronic constipation, bowel obstruction, and narcotic bowel syndrome are common complications of prolonged opioid use
  • Hormonal disruption: Opioids suppress the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, leading to hypogonadism, reduced testosterone, menstrual irregularities, and sexual dysfunction
  • Immune suppression: Opioid use impairs immune function and increases susceptibility to infections
  • Mental health deterioration: Depression, anxiety, and cognitive impairment frequently accompany chronic opioid use
  • Overdose and death: The introduction of fentanyl into the illicit drug supply has dramatically increased the lethality of opioid use disorder

Treatment Programs at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center provides structured outpatient treatment for opioid use disorder across multiple levels of care. Treatment plans are individualized based on each client’s clinical needs, substance use history, and co-occurring conditions.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

PHP provides the most intensive level of outpatient care, with structured clinical programming five to seven days per week. Clients receive daily individual therapy, group counseling, psychiatric evaluation, medication management including MAT, and comprehensive clinical monitoring. PHP is indicated for individuals transitioning from inpatient or residential care or those who require intensive support.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP provides structured treatment three to five days per week while allowing clients to live at home and maintain daily responsibilities. The program includes evidence-based individual and group therapy, MAT management, relapse prevention, and recovery skill development.

Outpatient Program (OP)

Standard outpatient care provides ongoing therapeutic support, medication management, and psychiatric services on a flexible schedule. OP is appropriate as a step-down from more intensive levels of care or for individuals with stable recovery who need continuing clinical support.

Medication-Assisted Treatment for Opioid Addiction

Medication-assisted treatment is the clinical standard of care for opioid use disorder and a cornerstone of treatment at Campus Recovery Center. MAT combines FDA-approved medications with counseling and behavioral therapies to provide a comprehensive approach to recovery. Research consistently demonstrates that MAT reduces opioid use, decreases overdose deaths, lowers the risk of infectious disease transmission, and improves treatment retention.

Medications used in opioid addiction treatment include:

  • Buprenorphine (Suboxone): A partial opioid agonist that reduces cravings and withdrawal symptoms without producing the full euphoric effects of opioids. Buprenorphine is the most widely prescribed MAT medication and can be administered in outpatient settings.
  • Naltrexone (Vivitrol): An opioid antagonist that blocks the effects of opioids entirely. Available as a monthly injection, naltrexone prevents relapse by eliminating the rewarding effects of opioid use.

Evidence-Based Therapies

Behavioral therapies are essential components of opioid addiction treatment and are delivered alongside medication-assisted treatment:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Helps clients identify triggers, develop coping strategies, and restructure thought patterns that contribute to opioid use
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): Teaches emotional regulation, distress tolerance, and mindfulness skills that are critical for managing the emotional challenges of early recovery
  • Contingency Management: Uses positive reinforcement to reward treatment adherence and sustained abstinence
  • Group Therapy: Provides peer support, accountability, and a sense of community that reduces the isolation common in opioid addiction
  • Motivational Interviewing: Strengthens internal motivation for change and helps resolve ambivalence about recovery
  • Trauma-Informed Care: Many individuals with opioid use disorder have histories of trauma. Our clinicians integrate trauma-sensitive approaches throughout all levels of care.

Recovery Outlook

Opioid use disorder is a treatable condition with well-established, evidence-based interventions. The combination of medication-assisted treatment and behavioral therapies has been shown to significantly improve outcomes. Studies demonstrate that individuals who remain in MAT for at least 12 months have substantially better long-term recovery rates than those who discontinue medication early. Campus Recovery Center supports clients through every phase of recovery, providing the clinical structure and therapeutic tools necessary for lasting sobriety.

Recovery from opioid addiction is achievable. With appropriate treatment, ongoing support, and commitment to the process, individuals can rebuild their health, relationships, and quality of life.
