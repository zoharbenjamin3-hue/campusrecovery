Alcohol use disorder (AUD) is one of the most common substance use disorders in the United States, affecting approximately 29.5 million people annually according to the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism. Despite its prevalence and the widespread social acceptance of drinking, alcohol addiction is a serious medical condition that can cause devastating physical, psychological, and social consequences. Campus Recovery Center provides structured outpatient treatment for alcohol addiction at our Joint Commission-accredited facility in Lake Worth Beach, Florida.

Understanding Alcohol Use Disorder

Alcohol use disorder is a chronic brain condition characterized by an impaired ability to stop or control alcohol consumption despite negative consequences. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) classifies AUD on a spectrum from mild to severe based on the number of diagnostic criteria a person meets. Alcohol affects the brain’s reward, stress, and executive function systems, creating neurological changes that make it increasingly difficult to moderate or quit drinking without professional help.

Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant that enhances the effects of the neurotransmitter GABA while inhibiting glutamate activity. Over time, the brain adapts to the presence of alcohol by altering its neurochemistry. When a person stops drinking abruptly, these adaptations can cause dangerous withdrawal symptoms, which is why medically supervised care is often necessary during the early stages of recovery.

Signs and Symptoms of Alcohol Addiction

Recognizing the signs of alcohol use disorder is an important first step toward treatment. Common indicators include:

  • Drinking more or for longer periods than intended
  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful attempts to cut down or control drinking
  • Spending significant time obtaining, consuming, or recovering from alcohol
  • Experiencing strong cravings or urges to drink
  • Failing to fulfill obligations at work, school, or home due to drinking
  • Continuing to drink despite recurring social or interpersonal problems
  • Giving up important activities or hobbies because of alcohol use
  • Drinking in physically hazardous situations
  • Continued use despite knowing alcohol is causing or worsening a physical or psychological condition
  • Developing tolerance—needing more alcohol to achieve the same effect
  • Experiencing withdrawal symptoms when alcohol use stops or is reduced

Health Effects of Chronic Alcohol Use

Prolonged alcohol misuse affects virtually every organ system in the body. The physical and mental health consequences of untreated alcohol addiction can be severe and, in some cases, irreversible:

  • Liver disease: Alcoholic fatty liver, hepatitis, fibrosis, and cirrhosis are progressive conditions caused by chronic heavy drinking
  • Cardiovascular damage: Chronic alcohol use increases the risk of cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, high blood pressure, and stroke
  • Neurological impairment: Alcohol damages brain tissue and can lead to cognitive deficits, peripheral neuropathy, and Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome
  • Gastrointestinal problems: Chronic drinking causes gastritis, pancreatitis, and increases the risk of esophageal and colorectal cancers
  • Immune system suppression: Heavy alcohol use weakens immune function, making the body more susceptible to infections
  • Mental health disorders: Alcohol use disorder frequently co-occurs with depression, anxiety, PTSD, and other psychiatric conditions

Treatment Programs at Campus Recovery Center

Campus Recovery Center offers multiple levels of outpatient care for individuals with alcohol use disorder. Our treatment team develops an individualized plan for each client based on the severity of their condition, co-occurring disorders, and personal recovery goals.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

Our PHP provides the highest level of outpatient care, with structured programming five to seven days per week. PHP is appropriate for individuals who require intensive clinical support but do not need 24-hour residential supervision. The program includes daily individual and group therapy, psychiatric evaluation, medication management, and clinical monitoring.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP offers structured treatment three to five days per week, allowing clients to maintain employment, attend school, or manage family responsibilities while receiving clinical care. The program includes evidence-based group and individual therapy, relapse prevention planning, and peer support.

Outpatient Program (OP)

Our standard outpatient program provides ongoing clinical support on a flexible schedule. OP serves as a step-down from IOP or as standalone treatment for individuals with less severe presentations. Services include individual counseling, group therapy, and psychiatric care.

Evidence-Based Therapies for Alcohol Addiction

Campus Recovery Center uses clinically validated therapeutic approaches to treat alcohol use disorder:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): CBT helps clients identify and change the thought patterns and beliefs that drive alcohol use. Research consistently demonstrates CBT’s effectiveness in reducing alcohol consumption and preventing relapse by developing healthier coping strategies and decision-making skills.
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): DBT teaches emotional regulation, distress tolerance, mindfulness, and interpersonal effectiveness skills. This approach is particularly valuable for clients who use alcohol to manage intense emotions or who have co-occurring personality disorders.
  • Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): Campus Recovery Center offers FDA-approved medications for alcohol use disorder, including naltrexone, acamprosate, and disulfiram. These medications reduce cravings, block the rewarding effects of alcohol, or create aversive reactions to drinking, and are most effective when combined with behavioral therapies.
  • Group Therapy: Facilitated group sessions provide peer support, reduce isolation, and allow clients to practice communication and interpersonal skills. Group therapy is a core component of all treatment levels at Campus Recovery Center.
  • Motivational Interviewing (MI): MI is a collaborative counseling approach that strengthens a client’s internal motivation and commitment to change. This technique is particularly effective during the early stages of treatment when ambivalence about sobriety is common.
  • Family Therapy: Alcohol addiction affects the entire family system. Our clinicians work with families to improve communication, establish healthy boundaries, and build a supportive home environment for long-term recovery.

Dual Diagnosis Treatment

A significant percentage of individuals with alcohol use disorder also have a co-occurring mental health condition. Depression, anxiety, PTSD, and bipolar disorder are among the most common co-occurring diagnoses. Campus Recovery Center provides integrated dual diagnosis treatment that addresses both the substance use disorder and the mental health condition simultaneously. Treating only one condition while ignoring the other significantly increases the risk of relapse and poor outcomes.

Recovery Outlook

Alcohol use disorder is a treatable condition. Research published in peer-reviewed journals consistently shows that individuals who engage in structured treatment programs have significantly better outcomes than those who attempt to quit on their own. Factors associated with successful long-term recovery include completion of a full course of treatment, engagement in aftercare and continuing support, use of medication-assisted treatment when clinically indicated, development of a strong sober support network, and treatment of co-occurring mental health conditions.

Recovery from alcohol addiction is a process that unfolds over time. Campus Recovery Center provides the clinical foundation for sustained sobriety while equipping clients with the tools and strategies they need to maintain their recovery long after treatment ends.
